Kickflips

1.

When the chair flew across the dining room I knew that it was time to go outside for a while. They had been fighting for the last several minutes, but I hadn't really been paying attention to what they were fighting about. I never did anymore. It was usually dad yelling about how no one appreciated him, no one gave a shit about him, and that all he ever did was work, work, work. It was true that he worked a lot, but it wasn't true that we didn't appreciate him. Dad's shouting was percussive, like always, and you'd absorb the shockwaves even if you were on the other side of the house. Mom pleaded her case as she struggled to hold back the tears. A door slammed and you would have thought that our brand new brick home was going to crumble. I tried my best to remain invisible.

It was a beautiful day to be outside. I mostly remember that it was warm and windy, and that I was trying to land kickflips in the driveway with little success. I had considered fishing. Our new neighborhood had a pond down the road, and that road would take me further away from all the yelling. I loved fishing, but I really wanted to land a kickflip. I danced with the skateboard a bit longer, and I had the bruises on my shins to show for it. Dad had a small burn pile over on the other side of the house where he had been clearing fallen limbs and leaves. The wind would occasionally blow smoke from what remained of the unattended smoldering ash in my direction, the scent attempting to usher in the beginnings of autumn, my favorite season, but it was still summer, and I still couldn't land a single damn kickflip.

Mom suddenly stumbled out of the house, anguished, dragging my younger brother by the wrist.

"John, get in the car. We're going to the store."

"What? Why? Do I have to go?" I grumbled. I wanted to keep practicing my trick, but as the words came out of my mouth I remembered that I didn't really want to be at the house alone with dad either.

"Just get in the car," she said without making eye contact with me. "Now!"

I rolled my eyes harder than I rolled the skateboard into the garage and climbed into the front seat of our brand new, silver Honda Accord. I buckled my seatbelt as mom helped Joey into his car seat. She got in the drivers seat, started the car, and we sat there in the garage for a few moments as the car idled. Mom searched for composure, and checked herself in the mirror on the back of the sun visor. She wiped her eyes and took a deep breath as Steven Curtis Chapman's comforting voice filled the empty space around us. She put the car in reverse, backed down the driveway, and started down the road. We were leaving.

2.

The streets in our subdivision were covered in mud. We had only lived there for a few months, and our house was among the first in the neighborhood to be completed. Everywhere you looked you'd see empty dirt lots with a tractor, a white van, piles of two by fours and plywood, or some combination of those things. In some of the dirt lots, the two by fours and plywood had already been arranged into the shape of an inordinate American dream—excessive, and designed for people who wanted to pretend that they had something they didn't. They were designed for us. Our new house was much larger than the one we had just moved from, and as a kid I assumed that this was nothing more than the natural progression of things.

Our neighborhood was a simulacrum of our entire town. We sat quietly in the car and listened to Steven Curtis Chapman for the next 20 minutes as we drove down what were once considered country roads, but were now blistered with capital-inducing strip malls, fast food restaurants, and gas stations.

3.

We arrived at the grocery store on the other side of town with no grocery list. It was the same grocery store we shopped at before we moved into the new house. As we entered through the automatic doors, mom grabbed a buggy, and Joey climbed right in. Mom barely noticed, still shaken by what had taken place earlier. The store was warm inside, which is unpleasant for a grocery store, and the lights were a little dim. There was a stench. There were angled mirrors all the way across the top of the back of the store, which made it so that you could see down every aisle from almost anywhere inside. Mom told us to stay close as we walked to the refrigerated section in the back of the store so that she could grab a gallon of skim milk. I preferred whole milk over skim milk, but I was told skim milk was healthier.

Joey, examining the gallon of milk that mom placed next to him in the buggy, asked, "mom, can we please get some cereal?"

"Yes, I was planning to get you some," she said. "We don't have anything for breakfast. Is there a kind that you'd like?"

"Fruit Loops!" he shouted with excitement.

I rolled my eyes as he said it. We had rules when it came to cereal, and Joey still did not seem to understand them.

"Joey, those have way too much sugar," mom said. "You're going to have to pick something else. How about some Cheerios? They're the same shape!" Mom's rule was that we couldn't get cereal that had more than ten grams of sugar per serving. This ruled out all of the good stuff, and was one of the more frustrating rules in our household.

We made it to the cereal aisle, and I started picking up boxes to read the labels on the back. I didn't even bother with the Cookie Crisps, or the CoCo Puffs, which would have been my top choices, and I remembered from last time that Reece's Puffs weren't an option either. Of course they weren't. Joey pointed to a box of Lucky Charms. Mom picked up the box, looked at the back of it, and set it back down. Frustrated, I picked up a box of Captain Crunch, the ones without berries. I looked at the label on the back.

"Sixteen grams of sugar! You've got to be kidding! They don't even have the berries!" I thought to myself.

I settled for a box of Corn Pops, which weren't my favorite, but they weren't the worst either. I always settled on Corn Pops. I reluctantly placed them and their mom-approved eight grams of sugar into the buggy. Mom grabbed a box of store brand Cheerios for Joey.

"What kind of Pop-Tarts do y'all want?" Mom asked. "I think we're out of those, too."

"S'mores!" Joey and I said at the same time. She knew the answer to this already. She grabbed a box so that we would have options for breakfast.

We moved towards the back of the store and she grabbed a couple pounds of ground turkey. She always said that turkey had less fat and that it tasted the same as ground beef. I disagreed, but I also didn't mind the turkey very much, so I didn't complain. We made our way around the outer edges of the store. We stopped and grabbed a loaf of wheat bread since we only had the two heels from the previous loaf at home. Even the fish didn't like wheat bread, but I knew white bread was out of the question. As we made our way back to the front of the store, we grabbed a box of Honey Buns and a case of caffeine-free Diet Coke, which were our regular options for after-school snacks.

There was no line at the only checkout lane that was open, and mom placed the skim milk, my Corn Pops, Joey's store-brand Cheerios, the Pop-Tarts, the ground turkey, the wheat bread, the caffeine-free Diet Coke, and the Honey Buns onto the conveyor belt. It wasn't until I saw our groceries sitting there, together, slowly gliding across the counter in front of me, that I realized how absurd all of this was. It was absurd that we couldn't have Fruit Loops, but we could have S'mores Pop-Tarts. We had to settle for skim milk but we could have caffeine-free Diet Coke and Honey Buns. It was absurd that mom and dad were fighting, and it was absurd that we moved away from all of my friends for no reason. It was absurd to repeatedly bruise my shins by trying to land kickflips, and it was absurd to catch a fish, to put it through so much unnecessary pain, only to immediately put it back.

4.

As we made our way home, we could hear the plastic grocery bags crinkling in the back floorboard as the car hummed along. We turned back into the neighborhood, passing by the dirt lots with their stacks of lumber. I looked out the window as we passed the pond and I thought about the fish that lived in it. I thought about what their lives were like, and I thought about whether or not their parents ever fought. I wondered if we had any hot dogs at home so that I could go catch some of them. I wondered if they could feel pain. I wondered what returning home would be like.

We came to the stop sign in front of our house, and I noticed that we were sitting there longer than we normally would. I turned my head away from the pond and towards our house, and was shocked to see that our grass, the entire front yard, was completely black. I turned again to look at mom and she was simply staring at the yard, speechless and defeated. She lowered her head onto the steering wheel.

"Oh my God, oh my God, oh my God," she whispered to herself. I could hear her, even though Steven Curtis Chapman was still filling space, though he was now unable to provide the comfort that the moment required.

Joey and I stayed silent. The wind had apparently expanded dad's burn pile. I swear to God that you could have laid a black cat in the yard and no one would have ever found it. By this point the fire was out, and the only other damage we could see was that some of the vinyl trim on the house and part of the roof had melted. Nothing structural. We were fortunate. We saw dad walking around the side of the house with the garden hose in his hand. He needed to look like he was doing something, like he was taking care of the situation, just like he worked so hard to take care of all of us, but it was unnecessary. It was all unnecessary.

I was nervous. I knew dad had a temper, and I figured the intensity of his emotions from earlier would only be exacerbated by his incinerating of our entire yard. We finally pull into the driveway and exit the car, unsure of what exactly to say. Cautiously, mom finally spoke.

"What on earth happened?" She asked in a tone that blended genuine concern with an understandable frustration.

"I don't really know. I threw a few more sticks on the fire, turned my back for a few seconds, and the yard was gone. It all happened so fast. I didn't know what to do. I grabbed the hose, but it didn't do shit. I guess a neighbor called the fire department, but by the time the truck arrived the fire was gone. It just stopped once it ran out of grass to burn. It all just happened so fast." Dad remained composed as he said all of this, much to my surprise. His words reflected a state of defeat, but also relief.

"Well, are you ok?"

"Yes, I am," Dad said. "Everything is going to be fine."

5.

I expected more fighting, but there wasn't any. It was in this moment that everything came to an end. There was no percussive shouting, and the brick walls of the house did not seem as if they were about to crumble. There was consolation, as if they were searching for a way to let each other know that they were each going to be ok. Love had been reduced to appreciation. There was appreciation for the parts of themselves that they had given to each other. There was appreciation for everything they had taught each other about themselves. There was an appreciation for Joey and I. There was an appreciation for growth. But there was resignation. There was an acknowledging of our new reality. The large house we now lived in felt smaller than you could ever imagine. We wouldn't be there much longer anyway. Once the grass had burned up, the siding had melted, the fire was out, and all that remained were the smoldering ashes of what once carried the scent of autumn into the warm summer air, there was no point in fighting anymore.

We stood there together in the driveway looking out over the charred remains of the grass. Joey suddenly began to cry. Mom picked him up, held him closely, and whispered into his ear that everything was going to be ok. She whispered this to him over and over again. I went into the garage, looked at my fishing pole for a few seconds, but instead grabbed my skateboard, returned to the driveway with everyone else, and continued to practice my kickflips.
