Jericho

I just remember that their chants were so loud. In that moment I'd say it was the loudest sound I had ever heard. We stood backstage, sweaty, bloody, for a few moments longer just to take it all in. The lights were dim in anticipation, the subtle hint of marijuana and cheap beer ever-present. I was emotional, of course, knowing that this was the last time that the four of us would experience this moment together. Any other night we'd barely pause to think about it, but now, their insistent plea, these three simple words, crashed down on us like the walls of a city that we constructed with our bare hands. "One more song! One more song! One more song!" Jericho.

The Cathedral was sold out that night. The lights danced and flickered with a calculated ferocity as the entire floor beneath us bounced like rubber. It was loud. The deafening cries of the fifty-two hundred illuminated faces made it nearly impossible to hear my own guitar. They were belting our songs back at us. It still blows my mind. It's hard to explain, but something spiritual happens. There's a consecration that takes place where your artwork, this delicately crafted piece of yourself, becomes nourishment, or perhaps even healing, to those who attend to partake. Body and blood. It's a paradox, being both god and man, at least for those two hours each night.

This was the culmination of the prior twenty years. You know how people talk about your entire life flashing before your eyes? Standing in that dark backstage hallway, I was suddenly everywhere all at once: our first show in Danny's basement, the show at that trailer park where Mark somehow busted his head open, the first time we played The Roundabout, at the table with Eccentric Records signing our first major deal, finding Matt in his hotel room, dead, a half-empty bottle by his side, the countless nights on buses, the hours at Geoff's studio, and, of course, that night on the sticky stage of The Cathedral. Every moment rushed back to the present, and I experienced them, in their fullness, all at once.

I stared down at my shoes as I held my guitar. It was all I could do. To acknowledge the moment, to acknowledge each other, would be to accept this ending, a spiritual death. This was church, and we had devoted a lifetime to our continuous prayer. A bead of sweat rolled down my cheek and splashed onto the floor as we processed onto the stage for the final time. "Atlanta!!!" This was 10 years ago.

Yesterday I was at the bank. I needed to deposit some cash I received after selling a guitar. I filled out the deposit form and I waited my turn in line.

"Next!" The teller waved me over. She was friendly enough.

"Hey, how's it goin?"

"Hangin' in there," she said.

"Just need to make a deposit." I handed her the slip and the cash.

She took the slip and started entering my information. She read my name and paused for a moment.

"Do I know you from somewhere? Your name sounds familiar."

"Hmmm. Not sure," I said.

We stood there quietly for the next thirty seconds or so before she handed me a receipt for my transaction.

"Alright! You're all set! Have a great rest of your morning."

"Thank you! You do the same."

I got into my car, drove across the parking lot, sat at the red light, and waited for it to turn green. I noticed the boarded up church across the street, the grass overgrown and the roof falling in, but I couldn't for the life of me remember how long it had been that way.
